# Blockchain Technology for Homeless Service Coordination

**Affiliation:** University of Washington School of Medicine / Washington State University

## Overview

This paper examines blockchain technology as a solution to coordination challenges in homeless service delivery. Homeless populations require access to diverse services (healthcare, food assistance, shelter, mental health support) provided by fragmented organizations across multiple jurisdictions. Current centralized database solutions face political, operational, and privacy barriers that prevent effective nationwide coordination.

## Key Concept

Blockchain's distributed network architecture enables:
- **Nationwide deployment** without centralized administration or political barriers
- **Universal accessibility** via standard smartphones and internet-connected devices
- **Privacy preservation** through cryptographic security while enabling appropriate data sharing
- **Real-time service gap identification** across organizational and geographic boundaries
- **Reliable auditing** for outcome assessment and resource optimization
- **Rights preservation** including secure digital identity for voting access and credential documentation

## The Problem

A homeless individual may visit a food bank in one city, receive emergency care in another jurisdiction, and seek shelter through a faith-based organization in a third location. Each organization maintains separate records, creating information silos that prevent comprehensive understanding of needs and service history. This fragmentation results in:

- Duplication of services while other needs remain unmet
- Inability to track service utilization across jurisdictions
- Critical gaps in essential care that go unidentified
- Inefficient resource allocation
- Compromised continuity of care

## The Solution Framework

Blockchain technology creates an immutable, transparent, and decentralized ledger that securely stores and shares information among multiple parties without requiring a central authority. The distributed consensus mechanism ensures data integrity while cryptographic security protects individual privacy.

Service providers across different cities, counties, and states can participate in the same network without ceding control to a single administrative entity, eliminating political and logistical barriers that have prevented nationwide coordination systems.

## Case Studies Reviewed

- **Blockchain for Social Impact Initiative** (UN Development Programme collaboration): Secure digital identities for refugees and homeless individuals
- **Binance Charity Foundation**: Blockchain platform for direct donor contributions with full transparency
- **Emerging registries** (Austin, Texas): Pilot blockchain-based homeless service registries
- **Healthcare applications** (BlockPres): Prescription management demonstrating transferable principles

## Implementation Considerations

The paper addresses practical challenges including:
- Technology access and digital divide issues
- Organizational capacity and training requirements
- Multi-stakeholder governance models
- Regulatory compliance (HIPAA and privacy regulations)
- Interoperability standards across diverse systems
- Ethical considerations (consent, autonomy, surveillance concerns)

## Target Populations

Primary focus on homeless populations generally, with specific analysis of homeless veterans as a potential implementation model. Veterans constitute approximately 5-13% of the homeless population and face elevated homelessness risk due to service-related trauma, transition difficulties, and inadequate support systems.

The framework's principles are broadly applicable to other vulnerable populations:
- Rural populations with limited service infrastructure
- Migrant workers moving across jurisdictions
- Individuals with severe mental illness
- Refugees and asylum seekers
- Foster youth transitioning between placements
